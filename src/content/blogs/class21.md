# News: RIP Sora 
## Presented by Team 3

Sora is an AI-generated video content platform, and the news team discussed how it was recently let go by OpenAI. Sora produces specific videos with AI prompts, and it was first released on social media and then as a standalone app. There are some inconsistencies in the videos Sora can generate, including incorrect generation of body parts or frames being cut off too fast. The CEO of the AI detection platform Copyleaks described Sora as a "content moderation nightmare." It spread rampant misinformation and could even make violent or racist content. OpenAI stated that they will still be using the video generation model for training content. The larger view of this is that this demonstrates an example of a limit placed on AI development which may emerge as a trend. 

## Should AI tools like Sora be released to the public if content moderation is not fully solved? Why or why not?

The class discussed being against technologies like Sora, mentioning other technologies such as Grok AI, which have pushed people away from video technologies. Some argue the Sora platform should be more heavily moderated, but if Sora videos are being uploaded on external platforms, then it is not Sora's responsibility to regulate it. 

# Lead: Model Collapse 
## Presented by Team 10

The lead team used a telephone as an analogy for model collapse. When a model collapses, the same information is repeated, and over time, it degrades and loses its original meaning. The concern with this is that AI-generated data is going to fall into future models' training data. The overarching issue the lead team brought up was that the model will start overestimating events that happen most often and this will lead to model collapse.

# Optional Additional Reading
ByteDance recently suspended a feature in its AI video generation model, Seedance 2.0. It was suspended after they discovered that the system could generate a person’s voice using only a facial image. In testing, the model could produce audio almost identical to a real person’s voice without requiring voice recordings or authorization. While the technology demonstrated impressive technical capability, it quickly raised serious concerns about privacy, identity theft, and deepfake abuse. If deployed, tools like this could enable scams, misinformation, or reputational attacks by allowing people to imitate others without consent. The consequences of such technology are endless unless there are guidelines and laws to follow. ByteDance temporarily disabled the feature and added new identity verification steps to reduce misuse. The case highlights the growing challenge of balancing AI innovation with ethical safeguards and responsible deployment.
